\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Appendix C. The Financial-Data Transparency Standard (Non-Operative)}
\usccrosshead{APPENDIX C. THE FINANCIAL-DATA TRANSPARENCY STANDARD (NON-OPERATIVE)}

\uscprov{1}{This appendix is non-operative. The prior bill established a
reusable standard for explaining how a piece of United States legislation was
created; this version establishes the matching standard for what an automated
process costs. The claim is narrow and demonstrable: a financial record is
transparent when it passes four tests - \textbf{completeness} (every cost
recorded at the moment it is incurred), \textbf{traceability} (every published
figure traceable to a record), \textbf{periodicity} (every series aligned to
the fiscal year), and \textbf{comparability} (every term used in the
controlled vocabulary of the Federal budget process)
\cite{kawchak2026vvuq05bill, gao-greenbook, gao-budget-glossary}.}

\uscprov{1}{The standard is demonstrated twice. First, by the bill's own
mechanism: section 515D(k) writes the cost of every verification run into the
part 11 hash-chained audit trail at the moment it is incurred (completeness,
traceability); section 5(a) publishes fiscal-year aggregates (periodicity);
and sections 5(d) and 515D(k)(1) speak in the GAO vocabulary (comparability).
Second, by this build's own development record: every milestone of the
auto-bill-02 process was committed and pushed in real time, every dollar
figure in every table carries its source or its illustrative marking, and the
commit schedule in Appendix E is itself a financial-grade ledger of the work
(graded against this standard in place). Figure 10 draws the system of
record; Table 12 maps the tests to the in-force instruments.}
